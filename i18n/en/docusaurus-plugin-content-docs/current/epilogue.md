## The Writing Process

After starting my career, I set up a personal blog and began sharing technical articles with netizens, mostly about the LabVIEW programming language. In 2009, I compiled those articles into a book and published it. Over the following years, I continued maintaining the book with revisions and expansions. However, as internet regulations grew stricter, many overseas websites were blocked, including my blog. And since I didn't have a domestic mobile phone number, I couldn't log in or leave comments on many domestic websites either. Communication with netizens dwindled, and I lost the motivation to write for a long time.

It wasn't until 2021 that I learned from a netizen that building a site on GitHub was becoming an important way to share knowledge. What was particularly valuable was that it hadn't been blocked domestically yet. So I moved that LabVIEW book to GitHub for maintenance ([https://lv.qizhen.xyz/](https://lv.qizhen.xyz/)). But by then, I hadn't used LabVIEW in years, and writing about it felt increasingly difficult. After all, my work primarily involved Python, so I thought, why not write about Python too.

This is a book that will only be published and shared online, so there's no deadline for completion. I will continue to refine and maintain it over a long period. However, before truly sharing it with readers, I wanted to quickly build a framework and complete a draft of the main content. Sharing something with nothing in it would be disappointing. My requirements for the draft were not very high: the content didn't need to be comprehensive, the structure could be unreasonable, and the language could be less than fluent—as long as it roughly reflected the overall architecture of the book. I originally thought it would take two or three months to prepare such a draft, but in reality, the draft took shape in just over a month. During that month, I spent one to two hours each day specifically on writing, sometimes even an entire day on weekends. I doubt I'll be able to find that much time again, but I will try to spend at least two hours per week maintaining and updating the book's content.

Although I set the bar extremely low for the draft, considering I could only write in my spare time, completing it in a month was very fast. Comparing it to my earlier book on LabVIEW, the same amount of writing would have taken at least two to three times longer. This is partly because LabVIEW itself is not conducive to writing. Unlike most programming languages, LabVIEW is not about writing code but "drawing" programs. This isn't necessarily a disadvantage for programming itself, but when you try to organize it into text documents, it is indeed more troublesome than text-based programming languages. However, the main reason that helped me speed up my writing was that this time I used a large language model to assist with the writing.

## Large Language Models

I have to admire the word-crafting skills of those experts in artificial intelligence (AI). Every few years, they come up with a new buzzword to refer to AI. When I first started learning AI, it was in its低谷 (low point): after decades of work, there were still few practical products. People didn't even like the term "artificial intelligence" anymore and replaced it with "machine learning" (ML). Not long after, everyone realized that among the many machine learning algorithms, the most promising were still multi-layer artificial neural networks. Later, "multi-layer" wasn't flashy enough—it had to be "deep"—so "deep learning" (DL) became popular as the representative of AI algorithms. In recent years, artificial neural networks have developed extremely rapidly, becoming deeper and deeper, from the initial few layers, to tens of layers, and quickly to hundreds or thousands of layers. Nowadays, the scale of a model is no longer measured by the number of layers but by the number of model parameters. Current mainstream large models for processing natural language have parameters ranging from tens of billions to trillions. For comparison, the number of neurons in the human brain is roughly between 50 billion and 100 billion. Models of such immense scale naturally disdain to be grouped with the old "deep" models, so the current buzzword has become "Large Language Models" (LLMs).

There is no doubt that OpenAI is the best in the field of large language models. Since I have always been involved in AI-related projects, I have paid close attention to every new OpenAI product. When they announced that they had added automatic code generation functionality to their GPT-3 large language model, a few friends and I couldn't wait to try it out. I don't remember the exact test questions anymore, only that they were random programming interview questions found online. One problem had an algorithm with simple code but poor runtime efficiency, and another algorithm that was extremely difficult to implement but had high runtime efficiency. We directly input the problem description into the GPT model, and it quickly responded with a simple, intuitive solution. When we asked if the algorithm could be optimized for better runtime efficiency, it generated a verbose but efficient program. We put its result into the compiler and tested it—it was completely correct. We were so shocked that my programmer friends and I were very worried about losing our jobs.

Upon carefully inspecting the code generated by GPT, we noticed a long and strange variable name, which was uncommon in typical programs. Curiously, we searched for this variable name and eventually found a solution to the same problem posted by someone else online, which was identical to GPT's output. This put me and my colleagues at ease—GPT was ultimately still "referencing" existing materials. When we tested it on problems unlikely to be found online, its performance was indeed much worse.

However, our relief didn't last long, because OpenAI soon launched ChatGPT, and its programming ability had significantly improved. At least when it comes to solving interview problems, I felt I could no longer surpass this AI model. Besides OpenAI's ChatGPT, I have also used Google's Gemini and Meta's LLAMA, among other large language models, and their performance has been excellent. I originally wanted to test several Chinese models from domestic companies, but unfortunately, they all had restrictions on overseas users at the time, preventing me from using them. Some models have now lifted those restrictions, and I can access them. I plan to test them thoroughly when I have the time.

## How Large Language Models Have Helped Me

The advancement of technology keeps us programmers on edge every day. "If you can't beat them, join them." So I decided to explore how to use large language models to assist my work.

Some time ago, I noticed that my company had already embedded large language models into the programming development environment. Now, when writing code, with every new line and every keystroke, the IDE suggests a ready-made code candidate. If you find the suggestion useful, pressing the Tab key automatically writes that snippet of code. Previously, mainstream IDEs also had some autocomplete features, but those were based on word prefix matching—for example, if you type an "a", it would ask if you wanted "add". This kind of suggestion was not only useless to me but also distracting. However, prompts based on large language models are on a completely different level; they can predict the code you need based on context. For instance, when you set a variable named `latitude`, it immediately realizes that your next line will likely be `longitude`; when you open a data table, it can guess that the next step is to read its data. The prediction accuracy of large language models is already very high, and the code candidates they provide have saved me a lot of typing.

I have also become increasingly dependent on large language models for learning. Previously, when I had a question I didn't understand, I would go to Google. But search engines have two serious flaws: first, they don't really understand my question; instead, they break it into keywords and search for articles based on those keywords. Second, when they return a large number of results, I still might not know which link is most relevant to my question, and sometimes I need to open multiple links to check. Even if I find the right article, the issue I care about might only be in a corner of that article, and I'd still need to read some unrelated content to find the information I need. Large language models can solve both of these flaws: I can directly ask a large language model a question in natural language, it will understand the entire question, and then directly give me the answer, rather than offering candidate links for me to analyze myself.

For some specific problems, the internet can hardly provide ready-made answers. For example, when a piece of code encounters a runtime error, if we search the internet for the error message, we might find some related articles, but it's almost impossible for any article to contain code exactly matching our problem. We still have to study the articles, understand the problem, and then apply it to our specific case. Once, I wrote a small program using PyTorch and encountered an error at runtime. At the time, I was too lazy to debug it myself and wanted to see if a large language model could handle this kind of problem. When I submitted my program and the error message together to the large language model, it not only pointed out the errors in my program but also generated the corrected code. The new code ran flawlessly.

For me, the most important help from large language models is not in programming but in English writing. To be blunt, I think my programming skills are decent. Without large language models, perhaps I would have a bit more advantage than most people. However, my English proficiency is somewhat lacking. After living in the United States for over a decade, I haven't improved much. English writing is particularly difficult; I often feel at a loss, and even I have trouble judging whether what I write is correct. Sometimes, I even had to ask my elementary school son to help me check. Since the advent of large language models, no matter what I write, I always ask it to help me revise first. It can not only correct errors in my language but also polish my writing to a level I could never achieve on my own. Now, I am considering translating the LabVIEW tutorial I wrote earlier into English. I hadn't mustered the courage to start this project before, mainly because I lacked confidence in my English skills, but now I am full of confidence.

Unfortunately, several large language models I have used are optimized for English, and their ability to handle Chinese is not yet sufficient to help me revise Chinese article content. However, even if their Chinese proficiency is not perfect, they are more than capable of doing some auxiliary work.

Throughout the process of writing this Python tutorial, I have also made full use of these large language models. The proverbial "the hardest part is starting"—with thousands of threads in mind, where should I begin? So, the first question I asked the large language model was: "I want to write a book about Python. What content should it include?" Its reply was: "Writing a book about Python is a great idea. Here are some topics you might want to include: 1. Introduction to Python: including Python's history, its main uses, and why it is a popular programming language. 2. Python Basics: such as variables, data types, operators, control flow (if statements, for and while loops), functions, error handling, etc. 3. Python Data Structures: such as lists, tuples, dictionaries, sets, etc. ..." I've only quoted a small part, but it listed all the common Python topics. It spoke with such conviction that I decided to follow its advice.

When writing the detailed content, I also made extensive use of large language models—to help me explain concepts, generate example programs, and so on.

## Problems with Large Language Models

Although large language models are powerful, they are not without flaws.

First, there is the issue with the models themselves: Large language models are not omniscient. Their knowledge comes from the internet. Although they can generate highly relevant continuations for prompt words based on the articles they have learned, and can recombine existing content, they cannot truly understand the logical relationships within questions, let alone explore entirely new domains. For problems that have never appeared before or for more complex logic, they can only fabricate answers out of thin air, and they usually do so with a straight-faced seriousness (hallucination).

For example, if you ask a large language model: "My wife was cheated on by her best friend. How should I comfort her?" If a real person heard this question, they might either condemn the scumbag or immediately realize it's a joke and continue with a humorous reply. But the AI model cannot understand the true meaning of this question. It will generate a formulaic response based on words like "cheated on" and "comfort," such as: "Faced with such a situation, the first thing is to understand and support your wife. Here are some steps that might help: 1. Listen and understand..." If this question appears on the internet many times in the future, the model might learn some more appropriate answers, but for now, it can only give mechanical responses.

The same goes for programming. Large language models excel at solving common interview problems because similar resources are already abundant online. But when faced with truly unique, new tasks, the models often struggle to provide satisfactory answers—they still make things up. They often write programs that look plausible, but when run, the results are completely wrong. As it stands, large language models are extremely useful auxiliary tools, but they cannot be fully trusted. Their answers cannot be applied directly to important situations without verification.

Second, I am also considering the impact of large language models on content creators. Traditional search engines do not present content directly to users; they only provide links, and users ultimately read the author's original text. Traffic gives authors the motivation to keep creating. But now, large language models can blend content from multiple articles and present it directly to users. Authors no longer know how many people have seen their work or whether it has been helpful. This is a fatal blow for creators, especially those who rely on traffic for income.

Large language models have greatly accelerated the completion of this book. However, from another perspective, they have also lowered the barrier to writing. If the labor of three months in the past can now be completed in one month, then the value of this work may only be a third of what it was, or even lower. In the future, we can foresee that the internet will be flooded with AI-generated articles, images, and videos, which will vastly enrich content while simultaneously diluting the value of most content.

Of course, as mentioned above, large language models cannot solve new problems or create entirely new content. In these areas, human experts are still essential. In the near future, large language models may eliminate some entry-level positions, such as junior programmers, junior writers, or junior artists. However, today's experts all started as beginners. Without these entry-level positions, how will we cultivate more high-level talent in the future?

Perhaps there is another possibility: AI's creative ability will also improve rapidly, reaching higher and higher levels. By the time human experts pass away, the successors won't be human beginners but AI itself—and there will be no so-called high-level talent anymore.

## Will Programmers Be Eliminated?

Looking back at the history of programming languages, the core trajectory is undoubtedly evolving towards "lowering the cognitive threshold for humans." From early punched tape, to obscure assembly language, to the popularization of high-level languages, humans have been building increasingly user-friendly layers of abstraction. Even among high-level languages, today's most popular language, Python, is known for its simplicity. Compared to C++, which was mainstream twenty years ago, Python has already significantly lowered the barrier to programming. Following this logic, in the near future, development in most application areas may no longer require traditional programming languages. Instead, users will simply express their needs clearly in natural language, and the computer will directly handle the logic construction and execution.

In the past, the biggest technical barrier to this ultimate goal was that machines could not accurately parse the ambiguity of natural language. However, the recent explosive breakthrough of large language models (LLMs) has essentially cleared this obstacle. "Natural language replacing programming languages" is within sight in many application scenarios. Of course, the profession of programmer will not completely disappear. Low-level architecture, core algorithms, and system development requiring extremely high performance will still need a small number of hardcore professional programmers. But we must face the reality that the demand for routine development positions will inevitably shrink significantly. Considering the massive pool of software talent accumulated during the industry's expansion over the past decade or two, the future supply-demand imbalance may confront ordinary programmers with an unprecedented employment winter.

The impact of this storm is not limited to programmers. Under the sweep of this round of AI technological innovation, teachers, secretaries, translators, painters, and even doctors, lawyers, and various types of drivers—none can remain unaffected. Although technological optimists always emphasize that "AI will also create new jobs," an extremely cruel reality lies before us: Can those who are ruthlessly eliminated by the technological tide easily cross that huge "skills gap" and complete a career transition without any pain?

Looking back from the threshold of the future, history is always strikingly similar. Every technological revolution hailed as a "great social progress" has often been accompanied by the squeezing out and elimination of large numbers of people at the bottom. The evolution of production relations always lags behind the狂飙突进 (rapid advance) of productive forces. In the early stages of major technological leaps, wealth and resources inevitably concentrate among the few who master the new technology, making life even harder for the majority who have not yet adapted to the change.

Faced with this irreversible technological tide, blind anxiety or resistance is futile. The core competitiveness of the future will no longer be proficiency in a specific language or a single professional skill, but rather the ability to deconstruct complex real-world problems, cross-disciplinary cognition, and the "architectural" ability to harness AI tools. For each of us, letting go of our dependence on existing skills as early as possible and attempting to transform from "executor" to "orchestrator and decision-maker" may be the only initiative we can hold onto in this brutal era of淘汰 (survival of the fittest).

## Will AI Become Conscious?

If the conclusion were that AI will soon become conscious and replace humans, then that conclusion would certainly be more attention-grabbing. It's no wonder that the machine threat theory (currently the intelligent machine threat theory) gets hyped every decade or so. But my view is still relatively conservative. I believe that at least within my lifetime, I will not see that day. Especially since humans themselves don't even know what consciousness is, how can we judge whether other entities have it? Discussing AI and consciousness nowadays easily deviates from technology and devolves into arguments about the concept of "consciousness" itself.

I don't have a deep understanding of what consciousness is, but I am quite familiar with AI. Currently, the most popular AI algorithms, including large language models and diffusion models, are all based on artificial neural networks. The core principle is to represent a complex function as a superposition of many, many simple small functions. We can abstract the problem to be solved into a function. For example, a function for chatting takes some text as input and outputs other text; a function for drawing takes some text as input and outputs an image. These functions are very complex, and we cannot express them with a single formula, but we can use methods similar to series expansion to decompose them into countless simple small functions. For instance, a large language model can be viewed as a complex function built by linearly superimposing hundreds of millions of extremely simple ReLU functions. Therefore, the essence of current AI algorithms is still function approximation, which doesn't seem to have much to do with "consciousness."

People who support the idea that AI will soon "awaken" mainly have two arguments. The first is that AI is a simulation of the human brain, so it should be able to have consciousness similar to humans. Indeed, the human brain is composed of neurons, and artificial neural network models are also composed of "neurons." However, I must reiterate that artificial neural networks are approximations of functions, not simulations of the human brain. The difference between artificial neural network models and the human brain is still significant. For example, at the most fundamental level, the neural network in the human brain is dynamic—neurons can form new connections or break existing ones; but the structure of artificial neural networks is static, fixed before training. The structures are also vastly different: the neurons in the human brain are truly networked and non-linear, while artificial neural networks are mainly hierarchically and linearly connected. In fact, our understanding of the human brain is still very limited; we haven't fully figured out its operating mechanism. Just because the human brain has neurons doesn't mean that human "consciousness" only involves neurons. Some scholars believe that quantum effects also participate in human thinking. Humans discovered quantum effects less than a hundred years ago. There may be many physical laws yet to be discovered that also participate in thinking activities. These are all things that current AI algorithms completely lack. Using the current simple structure of AI to simulate the human brain is still far from sufficient.

The second argument is that artificial neural networks have so many parameters—perhaps one day, they might suddenly flash with insight and develop self-awareness. This idea treats AI like magic. Current AI algorithms are entirely based on rigorous mathematical reasoning and computation; they possess no magical abilities. Making them conscious would still require active human design. Expecting AI to awaken on its own is somewhat like putting all the chemical substances that make up life into a bottle, shaking it, and expecting the molecules to arrange and combine themselves into a living organism. Sometimes we have beautiful wishes, hoping that things we don't understand can spontaneously produce magic and miracles. But in the end, these wishes almost always come to nothing.

In short, I am excited about the current progress in artificial intelligence. But as for AI developing complex intelligence similar to humans, I'm afraid it will likely take several more rounds of technological revolution.
